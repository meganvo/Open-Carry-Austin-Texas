Working with the National Football League (NFL), I designed an experience to increase fan engagement during the draft season. This experience let users visually explore which NFL Draft prospects top the Twitter rankings.

Adding Draft Picks

Typing triggers a fuzzy search to populate players. Once a player is selected, stats are generated.

"Mobile first design" aren't just industry buzz words

My initial iterations split up adding players between viewing the statistics. I thought it would be too cluttered to combine the two UIs.

However, I quickly learned after my initial mobile wires iterations that there was a lack of instant feedback validation once a user added players to the graph. Data would populate below the mobile viewport and it wasn't clear to the user that an action occured. With quick internal testing, I observed the cumbersome behavior of scrolling all the way down to view stats and then scrolling up to view the graph.

Data doesn't have to be daunting

Understanding data can be daunting, but it doesn't have to be. To help users make sense of it all, I focused my design efforts on designed a simple onboarding experience. It uses simple tooltips that appear on load for first time users.

Is 5,000 total tweets a lot? What about 50,000? It's hard to understand what numbers mean without context.

Working with a strategy team, I designed Twitter benchmarks comparisons to give user's a better sense of a player's popularity.
